Below is the query and analysis sequence for a case file concerning a "Tenant's Eviction Commitment Letter."

User Query #1

Analyze the validity of the eviction commitment letter I have and how I can evict the tenant based on this document.

VekilAI Analysis

Analysis initiated. The document and request are being evaluated within the framework of the relevant articles of the Turkish Code of Obligations.

Key Findings:

  • The requirement for the document to be in writing has been met.
  • The eviction date is clearly and explicitly stated.
  • The tenant's signature is present.

Potential Risks & Checklist:

  • Date of Issue and Eviction: The issue date of the commitment letter must be after the start date of the lease agreement. Otherwise, it can be argued that the tenant signed it under duress, which would render the document invalid.
  • Notarization: The lack of notarization places the burden of proof on you if the tenant disputes the signature.
  • Family Residence Annotation: If the leased property is a designated family residence, the explicit consent of the other spouse is also required for the commitment.

Strategic Recommendation:

If the tenant does not vacate the premises on the specified eviction date, you have the right to initiate enforcement proceedings or file an eviction lawsuit in the civil court of peace within 1 month from this date. This is a forfeiture period. Reference: TCO Article 352/1.

User Query #2

I am proceeding, taking into account the risks from your analysis. The document is not notarized and the date of issue was left blank. What should the strategy be in this situation?

VekilAI Analysis

Situation reassessed. A blank issue date can be interpreted in your favor according to the case law of the Court of Cassation.

Updated Situation Analysis:

  • Blank Issue Date: The Court of Cassation accepts that a subsequently filled-in issue date is valid (unless proven to be filled contrary to the agreement with written evidence). This weakens the 'signed under duress' claim.
  • Burden of Proof: Without notarization, the tenant has the right to deny the signature. In this case, a signature examination (graphological analysis) will be necessary.

Strategic Recommendation:

Choosing the path of enforcement proceedings can yield faster results. If the tenant does not object to the signature or debt within 7 days of notification, the proceeding becomes final. If they object, an action to annul the objection must be filed in the Enforcement Court. This action is typically resolved faster than a full eviction lawsuit. Reference: EBL Article 275.

User Query #3

The tenant has contested the signature. Strengthen my arguments for the action to annul the objection to be filed in the Enforcement Court. What evidence should I rely on?

VekilAI Analysis

Argument structure is being generated. The focal point is to prove that the signature belongs to the tenant.

Argument & Evidence List:

  • Request for Expert Examination: Request the court to obtain a graphological expert report comparing the signature on the commitment letter with the tenant's signatures on file with various official institutions (bank, notary, land registry). This is your strongest piece of evidence. Reference: CCP Article 211.
  • Rent Payments: Submit bank records of previous rent payments made by the tenant. This reinforces the existence of a tenancy relationship and thus the likelihood of signing such a document.
  • Witness Testimony (Limited Impact): If someone witnessed the signing of the commitment, they can be called as a witness. However, in a signature dispute, the expert report is more decisive.

Strategic Recommendation:

In your statement of claim, clearly state the request for a signature examination and declare your readiness to cover the costs. If the expert report is in your favor, the court will rule to annul the objection and order the tenant's eviction.

User Query #4

What are the potential counter-arguments the opposing party might use during this process? I want to be prepared for possible defenses.

VekilAI Analysis

Analyzing potential defense strategies of the opposing party.

Potential Counter-Arguments:

  • Claim of Mistake, Fraud, or Duress: The tenant may claim their will was vitiated when signing the commitment (that they were deceived or threatened). However, the burden of proof for this claim lies with them and requires concrete evidence.
  • Claim of Improper Completion: They may claim the issue date was filled in later and in bad faith. They can only prove this with other written evidence; witness testimony is generally insufficient.
  • Family Residence Claim: They could argue the property is a designated family residence and the other spouse's consent was not obtained. If proven (e.g., via official residence records), this could change the course of the lawsuit.

Counter-Strategy Recommendation:

Have the lease agreement, payment records, and any correspondence with the tenant ready. Emphasize that the opposing party's claims are abstract and lack proof. Against a family residence claim, check if the lease agreement contains any declaration to this effect and examine the land registry records.
